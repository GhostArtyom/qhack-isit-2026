    <td><p>Channel Estimation for Reconfigurable Intelligent Surface-Aided 6G NOMA Systems: A Quantum Machine Learning Approach</p></td>
    <td><p>The integration of reconfigurable intelligent surfaces (RISs) and non-orthogonal multiple access (NOMA) is considered a promising technique to enhance spectral efficiency and connectivity in future 6G networks. Accurate channel estimation remains a critical challenge in RIS-NOMA systems due to the increased complexity introduced by the combination of RIS and NOMA technologies. While quantum machine learning (QML) has demonstrated potential in wireless communications, its application in channel estimation remains underexplored. The results demonstrate that the proposed CNN-QLSTM model outperforms all baseline methods in terms of root mean square error (RMSE), mean absolute error (MAE), and mean absolute percentage error (MAPE).     <td><p>Digital Quantum Simulation of a Real Quantum Material: CoNb₂O₆ on IBM Quantum Hardware</p></td>
    <td><p>Understanding quantum phase transitions and correlated quantum behavior in real materials is a central problem in condensed matter physics. This work investigates the quantum simulation of spin systems through the quasi-one-dimensional magnetic compound CoNb₂O₆, which is modeled using the transverse field Ising model (TFIM). One-dimensional spin chains serve as paradigms for exploring critical phenomena, spin excitations, and entanglement in low-dimensional systems. The TFIM is particularly  
advantageous due to its analytical tractability and relevance to quantum criticality. In this study, CoNb₂O₆ is aligned to the TFIM framework with parameters informed by experimental results to reflect realistic spin-spin interactions and transverse magnetic fields. The Hamiltonian was decomposed using the Suzuki–Trotter method and implemented as quantum circuits in Qiskit 2.0, executed on both ideal simulators and actual IBM Quantum hardware (ibm-brisbane). Spin state evolution and dynamic responses near the critical point were examined, including the emergence of bound excitations and the role of quantum fluctuations. Comparative analysis between classical benchmarks and quantum simulation outcomes provides insights into the effects of noise, circuit depth, and current quantum processor limitations. Results indicate that present-day quantum devices can reproduce essential features of non-trivial spin models, demonstrating progress toward practical quantum simulation of condensed matter systems even before fault-tolerant hardware becomes available. This work highlights the potential of near-term quantum computation in bridging theoretical models with experimental systems.</p></td>

    <td><p>A Framework for Application-Layer Hybrid Post-Quantum Confidentiality.</p></td>
    <td><p>Harvest Now, Decrypt Later (HNDL) attacks let an adversary record encrypted traffic today and decrypt it once a cryptanalytically-relevant quantum computer (CRQC) becomes available. The cryptographic community has responded with hybrid post-quantum key exchange, but standards work and implementation effort have focused almost entirely on the transport layer, most notably TLS 1.3. This focus leaves a large class of confidentiality-critical data unprotected, including east-west traffic between services inside a trust boundary, payloads at rest in message queues, cached objects, and return values of internal function calls. We argue that effective HNDL mitigation also requires an application-layer approach that is independent of the transport. We present a framework with three properties: (i) transport-independent hybrid post-quantum confidentiality, (ii) composable granularity that lets developers protect entire sessions, individual calls, or specific return values under a single construction, and (iii) cryptographic agility through an adapter abstraction that decouples application code from the concrete post-quantum primitive. We then describe Kalyx-PQ, a Python reference implementation that combines X25519 with ML-KEM (NIST FIPS 203) via the Open Quantum Safe liboqs binding. Over 10,000 local handshakes, the hybrid handshake adds 26 to 39 percent latency over the X25519 baseline while remaining under 0.4 ms at the 95th percentile, with fixed key-material overhead scaling from 2,272 bytes (ML-KEM-768) to 3,136 bytes (ML-KEM-1024). An additional granularity cost analysis shows that selective application-layer hybrid PQ can be deployed without the all-or-nothing migration cost of transport-only approaches.</p></td>

    <td><p>Quantum Authentication using physical layer security</p></td>
    <td><p>Authentication plays a crucial role in ensuring the security of digital and physical resources for the communication system. Traditional authentication mechanisms, such as passwords and physical keys, face significant challenges related to practical usability and fundamental vulnerabilities. In contrast, biometric and PUF-based authentication leverage unique physical characteristics of devices to enhance security. By adopting PUF-based authentication, one can mitigate inherent issues associated with classical authentication systems, such as password forgetfulness and key loss or theft. Various classical models for PUFs have been proposed, such as optical PUFs, coating PUFs, arbiter PUFs, ring oscillator PUFs, and SRAM PUFs. These architectures being classical do not provide any robustness against quantum attacks and are vulnerable against machine learning attacks. In contrast, recent advancements have proposed exploiting quantum mechanical effects in certain devices to develop secure PUFs, termed Quantum PUFs (QPUFs). The inherent unclonability in the quantum domain significantly enhances the security of PUF architectures against quantum adversaries.</p></td>
